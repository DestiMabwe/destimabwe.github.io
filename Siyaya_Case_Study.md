# Siyaya Mobility Systems
### Delivery Lead — Turning an unvalidated build into a structured product decision

**Joining mid-flight**
I joined an early-stage mobility startup with a bold ambition: a foundational e-hailing platform that could eventually branch into multiple products, the way Uber's core has grown into Eats, Freight, and beyond. I wanted to be close to delivery — in the room with developers, not managing from a distance. They were already mid-build when I arrived, deep in the work of bringing something real to life.

**Formalizing, not imposing**
Rather than layering on a new framework, I formalized what was already in motion — turning scattered progress into a structured product roadmap and a sprint-ready backlog with clear epics and sizing. I also worked directly inside the GitHub workflow, refining issues and staying close enough to infrastructure conversations — comparing tools, weighing services, and thinking through setup — to act as a genuine technical thought partner, not just a process facilitator.

One gap stood out early: the team had a development environment but no proper staging environment before production. I flagged it as a delivery risk and pushed for the fix. Being a technical project manager first, scrum master second, I  refused staying at arm's length from the technical work — it meant writing issues that set developers up to succeed, removing impediments before they became delays, and asking the right questions even where I wasn't the expert in the room.

The MVP came together faster than I expected. That speed was exciting — and it's also when the real problems surfaced.

**Three things moving at once**
Market insight kept evolving as we learned more about the e-hailing space. The business model was still unsettled — subscription or commission, and how to stand out in an already crowded market. And regulatory approval requirements, still being mapped, added a layer of uncertainty none of us fully controlled.

**The gap underneath the speed**
Somewhere in that shifting ground, it became clear: the real issue wasn't the product. It was that we'd never agreed on who the first customer actually was. We had a platform. We didn't have a clearly defined market.

**Bringing it into focus**
I facilitated a full Business Model Canvas workshop — all nine building blocks, mapped and debated together — to force that clarity before we built further in the wrong direction.

**The principle behind it**
I wasn't running Scrum ceremonies in the traditional sense, but the fundamentals were exactly what got us here. Transparency: surfacing that we didn't actually agree on our first customer, instead of letting the assumption ride. Inspection: checking our progress against that gap often enough to catch it before more was built on top of it. Adaptation: pausing to run the workshop and course-correct, rather than pushing forward on an unvalidated plan. That's empirical process control in practice — not the ritual, but the discipline behind it.
